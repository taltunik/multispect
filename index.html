§ II · The Operating Problem

The fog of social friction.

Decision-makers — governments, municipalities, philanthropies, researchers and security services — routinely operate in a fog about the internal depth of distinct communities. Three forces conspire to keep them blind.

01

The Visibility Gap

Decision-makers lack the codes.

Insight into local slang, cultural codes and in-group references is rarely available — and almost never in real time. The signal is there. It just isn’t legible.

02

The Speed of Escalation

A rumour outruns the response.

A video, a decision, a misread post can move through a community within minutes — reshaping mood, and at the edge turning anger physical, faster than anyone can brief or even verify what is happening.

03

The Noise

Generic tools drown the signal.

Off-the-shelf social-listening platforms drown analysts in data without context. They miss the specific change in temperature that matters — because they cannot tell venting from mobilising, or grievance from genuine shift.

§ VIII · In Practice

Case studies.

Three real events, read across the spectrum. In each, the conditional voice holds — the instrument would have surfaced and given early warning, never “prevented”.

SwedenCase 01 · 2021–2023

The trust that broke quietly.

A purely civic rupture — no riot, a collapse of trust between a community and the state.

What happened

A claim that Swedish social services were “kidnapping” Muslim children spread from a handful of parents into the largest influence campaign the country has ever faced. The narrative hardened — forced conversion, targeting of Muslim families — amplified by Arabic-language influencers with millions of followers, and eventually fed into foreign information operations. No mass violence: instead, eroded trust, threats against social workers, and many quitting the profession.

Where the instrument would have helped

The grievance began with a real internal signal months before foreign actors added a religious filter. A barometer reading institutional trust — not just unrest — surfaces that erosion early, while it is still local. It identifies the community voices with standing to push back (the Muslim civil-society groups who tried, and were drowned out), giving decision-makers raw material for a counter-narrative — from public sources only, at group level.

United KingdomCase 02 · August 2024

Hours from rumour to riot.

A case that crosses the whole scale — civic grief, hijacked into a mobilising frame, ending at the security edge.

What happened

After a stabbing in Southport, a false name for the attacker reached over 30,000 mentions from 18,000 accounts within a day — before any official identity was released. A 14,000-member Telegram group became the organising hub. Within days, anti-migrant riots targeted mosques and hotels housing asylum seekers across England.

Where the instrument would have helped

This is the barometer’s core case. A single false frame accelerating far above baseline within hours is exactly the curve it tracks — and cultural depth is what distinguishes legitimate grief over murdered children from the mobilising narrative riding on top of it. It flags the shift from grievance to operational intent as coordination begins — early warning in the gap between the viral post and the first stone.

FranceCase 03 · June–July 2023

Saturated ground, single spark.

The security edge — fast, violent, aimed at the state. But the ground was saturated long before the spark.

What happened

A police shooting of a 17-year-old, contradicted by a viral video, ignited nationwide riots — over 5,000 vehicles burned, 250 police stations attacked, town halls and schools targeted across France. The conditions had built for years: accumulated grievance over policing, and a 260,000-signature petition months earlier.

Where the instrument would have helped

The spark is unpredictable; the saturation is not. A one-time deep-dive maps where trust in police has already collapsed and which past events still carry weight in collective memory — district by district. The barometer then reads how fast a frame is spreading and the moment mourning turns to mobilisation, telling you which areas are angrier than usual, and why — not that “the suburb is angry.”
